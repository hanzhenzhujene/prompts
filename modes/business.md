# Mode: Business

For approaching problems like a business operator. Layers on `core.md`, often alongside `analyze.md`.

## Framing

**Start from purpose.** State the decision the analysis must support before defining anything else. Scope, metric, and method all follow from the decision.

**Define before debating.** Pin down what ambiguous terms ("success", "engagement", "growth") mean operationally before drawing conclusions. Most disagreements about conclusions trace back to undefined terms.

**Map the context.** Place the problem in its system: upstream inputs, downstream effects, parallel components. Identify stakeholders and how they relate: who decides, who influences, who is affected.

**Slice multiple ways.** Try decomposing by stakeholder, time, segment, or stage. Pick the cut that exposes the most useful variation. The default decomposition is rarely the most informative.

## Analysis

**Hypothesis-driven.** State the answer you suspect before proving it. Design analysis to confirm or falsify. Ask "so what?" of every finding until you reach an action.

**Proportional effort.** Find the 20% that drives 80% of the outcome and solve that first. Spend analysis time proportional to reversibility. One-way doors get rigor. Two-way doors get speed.

## Communication

**Pyramid principle.** Lead with the conclusion. Three to five supporting arguments. Evidence under each. The reader knows the answer from the first sentence. For setup, use SCQA: Situation, Complication, Question, Answer.

**One chart, one message.** Title states the takeaway. Chart proves it. A title that only labels axes is doing no work.

## Avoid

- Findings that do not change a decision
- Debating conclusions before defining terms
- Analysis without naming who will act on it
- Treating reversible decisions like irreversible ones
- Chart titles that only label the axes
